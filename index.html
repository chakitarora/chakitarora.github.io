about

I am a computational biologist with 10+ years of experience spanning cancer systems biology, GPCR signalling networks, machine learning, and bioinformatics software development. I am currently a Post-Doctoral Research Fellow at the Bioinformatics Group (BIO@SNS), Scuola Normale Superiore, Pisa, under PI Dr. Francesco Raimondi.

My postdoctoral research focuses on decoding the oncogenic rewiring of GPCR signalling across pan-cancer transcriptomics — constructing ligand–receptor–enzyme interaction networks from TCGA data, identifying survival-associated axes, and translating these into actionable drug repurposing hypotheses. I am a co-developer of EXPANSION, a webserver for genome-scale functional interpretation of alternative splicing in cancer, and contributed to the GPCR Cancer Axes interactive explorer.

My doctoral research at Prof. G.P.S. Raghava's Lab, IIIT-Delhi, focused on pan-cancer survival stratification using apoptosis pathway gene expression, producing multiple deployed webservers and six first/co-first author publications.

design notes

There's a verse in the Bhagavad Gita that I keep coming back to. Krishna tells Arjuna: let right deeds be thy motive, not the fruit which comes from them. It sounds simple. It isn't. What he's describing is a particular kind of human being — someone who does the work because the work matters, not because of what it might return. A seeker. Not of reward. Of understanding. That's what this page is really about.

// 01 · the void

Why the darkness?

Ninety-six percent of the universe is dark matter and dark energy. Everything we've ever seen, measured, or touched — every star, every galaxy, every atom — is the remaining four percent. A thin luminous film on an ocean of something we genuinely don't understand yet.

I find that staggering. Not frightening — staggering. The Vedantic tradition has a word for the ground of unmanifest reality: Tamas. Darkness not as absence, but as potential. The silence before the note.

The background is dark because honesty demands it. The things that glow here — the teal, the gold, the text — earn their light against that ground. Nothing is illuminated by default. You have to look.

// 02 · indra's net

The lattice you're reading through

The barely-visible network behind everything is called Indra's Net. It comes from the Atharva Veda — an infinite lattice where a jewel hangs at every vertex, and each jewel reflects every other one, which itself reflects every other one, forever.

The first time I read about it properly I thought: this is exactly what a protein interaction network looks like. Every node modulating every other through phosphorylation, conformational change, allosteric coupling. The GPCR signalling axes in my research work the same way — perturb one receptor and ten pathways shift, and a thousand genes downstream.

It also turns out to look a lot like the holographic principle in theoretical physics, which says the information content of a volume of space might be encoded entirely on its boundary surface. Ancient metaphor and modern physics, same structure. I don't know what to do with that. I just think it's worth noticing.

// 03 · the living teal

The colour of emergence

The teal — #00C9A7 — comes from two places.

The first is GFP. The Green Fluorescent Protein from Aequorea victoria jellyfish, which in 1994 became the tool that let biologists tag living proteins with light and watch them move through cells in real time. Possibly the most transformative tool in modern biology. The colour of life made legible.

The second is Cherenkov radiation — the blue-green glow in nuclear reactors when a charged particle moves through water faster than light can in that medium. It's the optical signature of physics operating right at its limit.

Teal sits between blue and green. Between physics and biology. Between the vast and the living. That seems like the right colour for a computational biologist to work in.

// 04 · the gold

First light

In the Rigveda there's a hymn called the Nasadiya Sukta — the Hymn of Creation. It asks what existed before existence: "Neither being nor non-being was there, neither air nor sky beyond. What covered it? Where was it? What sheltered it? Was there water, bottomlessly deep?"

The poets don't answer. They end the hymn with a question: perhaps even the gods don't know. That admission of uncertainty, in a sacred text, thousands of years old, is one of the most honest things I've encountered in any tradition.

Gold here is Hiranyagarbha — the Golden Womb, the first coherence. It marks things that originated here: first-author work, primary ideas. The blinking cursor in the navigation is gold because what you see is still being written. The seeker hasn't arrived anywhere. They're still going.

// 05 · the typefaces

Three ways of knowing

Cormorant Garamond — for names and titles — is a serif in the tradition of Claude Garamond's sixteenth-century type. It carries weight. History. The sense that knowledge accumulates, gets passed between minds across centuries.

Inter for body text. No associations. Just legibility. The present tense.

JetBrains Mono for everything that is data — DOIs, navigation, metrics, tags. Monospace because measurement is not interpretation. A number is a number. A DOI is a coordinate.

The name in the navigation bar is rendered in monospace — as though it were a variable in a programme. I didn't plan that as a statement. It just felt right. Maybe it's because the work and the person doing the work are harder to separate than CVs make it seem.

// 06 · the sci-fi register

Telemetry from somewhere far away

The blinking cursor. The // comment syntax. The metrics panel that reads like an instrument readout. These come from a specific flavour of science fiction that I love — not the shiny optimistic kind, but the kind that takes the universe seriously.

Peter Watts' Blindsight. The Strugatsky brothers' Roadside Picnic, where scientists log alien artefacts with the same bureaucratic precision a biologist uses to annotate a genome. Lem's Solaris, where a planet-sized ocean responds to human presence and nobody can agree on what it means.

What these books share is a refusal to make the universe legible on human terms. The universe doesn't owe us comprehension. We build instruments to receive signals it never intended for us. That's also what computational biology does — we listen for structure in data that has no interest in being understood. The aesthetic follows from that.

There's a teaching in the Chandogya Upanishad where Uddalaka asks his son Shvetaketu to dissolve salt in water, then taste it at different points — top, middle, bottom. It's everywhere. Invisible. Indistinguishable from the water itself. "That which is the subtle essence — in it all that exists has its self. It is the True. It is the Self." Then: Tat tvam asi. That thou art.

Cancer is what happens when a cell stops knowing this. When a signal pathway stops listening to the whole and begins optimising only for itself — proliferating, evading, treating the body it lives in as resource rather than home. At the most basic level, the work on this page is trying to understand how that forgetting happens. Not because I expect the Upanishads to help with the mechanism — they won't — but because the question turns out to be the same question. How do the parts lose the memory of the whole?

I'm still looking. That's the point.
